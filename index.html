About This Guide

What's in the Guide

Six carefully selected online resources covering reference tools, free courseware, professional organizations, structural calculators, materials databases, and career development platforms relevant to engineering undergrads.

Who It's For

Undergraduate engineering students at Penn State University Park, particularly those in or preparing for core engineering courses like Statics, Dynamics, Mechanics of Materials, and Thermodynamics.

What It Assumes

Readers have a basic familiarity with engineering concepts and are comfortable navigating websites independently. No prior knowledge of any specific platform covered in this guide is required.

How It's Organized

Resources are grouped by function: reference tools, learning resources, professional organizations, calculation software, databases, and career tools. Each entry includes a full abstract and usage tips.

Tips for Using This Guide

Use the table of contents to jump directly to a resource. Each abstract answers what the resource is, what's in it, how it helps you specifically, and how to use it efficiently. Start with the resources most relevant to your current coursework.

The Resources

Six Essential Sites for Engineering Students

Each resource below is described with its content, relevance to Penn State engineering undergrads, and tips for getting the most out of it.

1

The Engineering Toolbox

engineeringtoolbox.com
Reference

The Engineering Toolbox is an invaluable tool made for engineers and provides a wide range of tools, calculators, and information resources. This website can be used for a multitude of subjects including fluid mechanics, material properties, HVAC systems, electrical engineering, and more. It can be used as a faster and more effective version of a textbook as it is searchable, so finding that formula or constant doesn't take fifteen minutes.

For Penn State students this website will be most helpful during the required mechanics classes including EMCH 211 (Statics), EMCH 212 (Dynamics), and EMCH 213 (Strength of Materials). Another class I have used Engineering Toolbox for is Thermodynamics to find specific heats and enthalpy ratios for certain materials. That's the great thing about this website — when I need to know the shear stress formula or the Elastic Modulus of a material, Engineering Toolbox gets me there in seconds. The site presents data as stand-alone reference material rather than guided lessons, so it works best as a lookup tool rather than a learning resource.

How to use it efficiently

Go straight to the search bar and search "Elastic Modulus," or "Shear Stress Formula." Each page will include some combination of tables, graphs, and formulas. It will also have links to related concepts if the problem is covering multiple topics.

Example: Young's Modulus page
2

MIT OpenCourseWare - Engineering

ocw.mit.edu
Courseware

MIT OpenCourseWare (OCW) is a free archive of actual course materials from MIT, including lecture notes, problem sets, exams, and posted solutions from hundreds of engineering courses. It's maintained by MIT and covers everything from introductory mechanics to advanced structural analysis, making it one of the most legitimate free academic resources available to any engineering student.

For Penn State undergrads, OCW is most useful when your own course materials aren't cutting it. If a concept from EMCH 213 or Dynamics isn't clicking after lecture, MIT's notes and worked examples will often explain the same thing from a completely different angle. The posted exams with solutions are especially good for test prep — they give you extra practice problems beyond what your professor assigns, and they're at a comparable difficulty level to what you'll actually see on an exam.

How to use it efficiently

Search by topic rather than browsing by department - typing "mechanics of materials" or "beam bending" pulls up relevant courses immediately. Once you're inside a course, go straight to the Assignments or Exams section, that's where the useful stuff lives. Don't try to follow an entire MIT course — just pull the specific lecture or problem set that covers what you're stuck on.

3

American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE)

asce.org
Professional Org

The American Society of Civil Engineers is the main professional organization for civil and structural engineers in the United States, with over 150,000 members worldwide. The website covers industry news, technical standards, continuing education, student resources, and career tools — basically everything you'd need to understand what engineering looks like as an actual profession rather than just a set of courses.

For Penn State students, ASCE is useful in two main ways. First, it has student chapter resources and membership information that show you what professional engineering looks like beyond the classroom, including conferences and competitions you can actually participate in as an undergrad. Second, the ASCE Library gives access to peer-reviewed technical papers and journals, which you'll need if you're doing a capstone project, honors thesis, or any assignment that requires citing real engineering research rather than just your textbook.

How to use it efficiently

Create a free student membership account first - it unlocks discounted access and student-specific content. Use the "Students and Early Career" section as your entry point rather than trying to navigate the full site, which is mostly built for working professionals. For journal articles, access the ASCE Library through Penn State's library system to avoid hitting paywalls.

4

SkyCiv Structural Engineering Software

skyciv.com
Calculator

SkyCiv is a cloud-based structural engineering platform with a free beam calculator, section builder, and structural analysis tools built right into your browser - no software to install. You plug in your beam, apply loads, and it generates shear force diagrams, bending moment diagrams, and deflection curves instantly. It covers the exact type of problems you'll spend a lot of time on in EMCH 213 and structural analysis courses.

The most practical use for Penn State students is checking your hand calculations. After you work through a beam problem manually, you can input the same setup into SkyCiv and see if your shear and moment diagrams match. If they don't, something went wrong in your work and you can catch it before it costs you points. It's also just a good way to build intuition for how different support conditions and load placements change the behavior of a beam - something that's hard to visualize from equations alone.

How to use it efficiently

The free beam calculator requires no account - just go to the site and start entering your beam parameters. Always do the problem by hand first and use SkyCiv to verify, not replace, your work. You won't have it on exams. If you want to explore 3D frame analysis or more detailed section properties, a free account unlocks more features without paying anything.

5

MatWeb Material Property Database

matweb.com
Database

MatWeb is a free online database with material property data for over 175,000 engineering materials - metals, polymers, ceramics, composites, you name it. Each entry lists the mechanical properties that actually matter for engineering work: tensile strength, yield strength, modulus of elasticity, density, thermal properties, and more. The data comes from manufacturers and published engineering references, so it's reliable enough to cite in a formal report.

For Penn State engineering students, MatWeb solves a very specific and recurring problem - finding accurate material properties without digging through appendices in the back of your textbook. When an assignment gives you AISI 1020 steel or 6061-T6 aluminum and asks you to calculate stress or deformation, MatWeb has the exact values you need in seconds. It's also useful for design courses where you need to compare multiple materials and back up your selection with actual numbers rather than just saying one material "seems stronger."

How to use it efficiently

Search by material name or alloy designation directly from the homepage. When results come up, make sure you select the entry that matches your specific condition - "annealed" vs. "cold drawn" steel have different property values and using the wrong one will throw off your calculations. Each property entry shows its source, so you can cite it properly in a lab report or design project. A free account lets you save materials to a comparison list, which is handy when you're evaluating multiple options for a design problem.

6

Handshake

joinhandshake.com
Career

Handshake is a career platform built specifically for college students, connecting them with employers who are actively recruiting from universities. Penn State has an institutional partnership with Handshake, meaning the version you access through Penn State is already filtered for companies targeting Penn State students - it's not just a generic job board where your application disappears into a pile.

For engineering students, Handshake is the most direct path to internships and entry-level positions from companies that have already decided they want to hire from Penn State. Unlike LinkedIn or Indeed, you're not competing with the entire internet - you're competing with other Penn State students for roles that were posted specifically because the employer recruits here. The platform also lists on-campus recruiting events, career fairs, and employer info sessions, which are some of the best ways to actually get your foot in the door before you ever submit a formal application.

How to use it efficiently

Access it through Penn State's Engineering Career Resources page using your PSU login. Fill out your profile completely - major, graduation year, GPA, relevant coursework - because employers filter candidates using exactly those fields. Set up job alerts for your specific discipline so you're not manually checking the site every day. Start using it early, even freshman or sophomore year, just to attend employer events and get a feel for what skills companies are actually looking for before you're actively job hunting.
